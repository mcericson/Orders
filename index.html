Orders

The classical column is an architectural element that is impossible to ascribe it to a single architect. Vitruvius is the first architect to describe the classical orders—Doric, Ionian, and Corinthian—but his role is as a documentarian not a designer. Similarly, Alberti set out a numerical system for defining the base, shaft and capital of the column. In both Alberti’s and Vitruvius’s writing, there are no illustrations—Vitruvius’s were lost and Alberti intentionally used only text. Instead, the reader is left with textual descriptions in the form of proportional rules that define the shape and order of the column. These rules allowed the column to be designed at a variety of scales and sites, while maintaining a consistent set of formal relationships. The rules also prioritize shared knowledge over the individual designer or craftsperson, by exposing a detailed process for its reproduction. An Ionic column on a small temple will have twenty-four flutes defined by the carpenter’s square in the same manner as a larger Ionic column in a larger temple. In this instance, the design process is embedded in the order of operations defined in relationship to a tool, the carpenters square. Exposing the rules of the order not only ensures its replicability but also its potential to be transformed and reimagined. This semester, we will study columns and their ordering systems as a means of developing a set of shared tools and collaborative methods across the studio. The studio will begin with a detailed precedent analysis of the column and move onto the reimagining of its rules and systems to produce new Orders of columns and eventually building proposals derived from this initial research.
